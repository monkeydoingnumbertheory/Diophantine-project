1. Introduction

In this article, we aim to provide the reader with a taste of Diophantine approximation, closely following the book Exploring the Number Jungle by Edward B. Burger, and some occasional references to the classical book An Introduction to the Theory of Numbers by Hardy and Wright.

Note:
Since the book is more of an exercise book, most of the proofs, unless stated otherwise, have been done by the author along with help from the supervisor. Hence, the proofs may not be the most elegant, and all errors are most certainly the author's.

Firstly, what is Diophantine Approximation, and why do we even bother with it?

Diophantine approximation is the study of how well real numbers can be approximated by rational numbers. This field connects number theory, analysis, and geometry, providing deep insights into the distribution of rational points and the structure of real numbers.

2. Farey Sequences

2.1 Properties of the Farey Sequences

Here, we uncover the arithmetic structure of the Farey Sequences. Most of the observations here were made by J. Farey in 1816. However, he gave no proof. Cauchy, however, immediately provided the proofs after seeing Farey's statement.

Definition 2.1 (Farey Sequence)
A Farey Sequence of order N is the ordered list of all reduced fractions between 0 and 1 having denominators not exceeding N. We denote this sequence by \(\mathfrak{F}_N\).

So for example:

\begin{align} \mathfrak{F}_1 &= \left\{ \frac{0}{1},\frac{1}{1} \right\} \\ \mathfrak{F}_2 &= \left\{ \frac{0}{1},\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{1} \right\} \\ \mathfrak{F}_3 &= \left\{ \frac{0}{1},\frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{2},\frac{2}{3},\frac{1}{1} \right\} \end{align}

We take a slight detour, first considering the size of \(\mathfrak{F}_N\), before considering its arithmetical structure.

Let \(\#(\mathfrak{F}_N)\) denote the number of elements in \(\mathfrak{F}_N\). For example, \(\#(\mathfrak{F}_1)=2, \#(\mathfrak{F}_2)=3, \#(\mathfrak{F}_3)=5, \#(\mathfrak{F}_4)=7\) and so on. Looking at the first few values for \(\#(\mathfrak{F}_N),\) one might conjecture that \(\#(\mathfrak{F}_N)\) is always prime. But this is in fact not true.

Lemma 2.1
For a positive integer \(N \geq 2,\) the number of elements in \(\mathfrak{F}_N\) having denominator \(N\) is equal to \(\varphi(N),\) where \(\varphi(N)\) is the Euler totient function.
Proof:
By Definition 2.1, the elements with the denominator \(N\) are precisely the fractions that have numerators which are coprime to \(N,\) and the number of them is given by \(\varphi(N).\) □
Proposition 2.1
For any \(N\geq2,\) \[ \#(\mathfrak{F}_N)=1+\sum_{m=1}^{N}{\varphi(m)} \]
Proof:
The statement is true for \(N=1.\) Assuming that it is true for \(N=n,\) the number of elements that are in \(\mathfrak{F}_{n+1}\) is the sum of the number of elements in \(\mathfrak{F}_n\) and the new elements with numerator coprime to \(n+1\) and denominator \(n+1,\) of which there are \(\varphi(n+1)\) of them. □

Using this formula, we check that \(\#(\mathfrak{F}_{10})=33,\) which disproves our conjecture.

Remark:
While there does not exist a simple, closed formula for the formula in the previous proposition, the simple function \(f(N)=\frac{3}{\pi^2}N^2\) approximates the formula surprisingly well for sufficiently large \(N.\) For example, \(\#(\mathfrak{F}_{10})=33\) while \(\frac{300}{\pi^2} \approx 30.4\). The reader might recognise this particular function to be the average order of the Euler totient function.

We now go back to uncovering the underlying arithmetical structure of the Farey Sequences. From here onwards, we shall assume that all rationals discussed are positive, along with the natural numbers \(p,q,r,s, a,b,x,y\).

Lemma 2.2
Let \(\frac{p}{q}<\frac{r}{s}\) be two rational numbers satisfying \(ps-rq=-1.\) Then for all positive integers \(\lambda \text{ and } \mu,\) we have \[ \frac{p}{q}\leq\frac{\lambda p +\mu r}{\lambda q + \mu s} \leq \frac{r}{s} \]
Proof:
We have that the statement is equivalent to: \begin{align} &\frac{p}{q}\leq\frac{\lambda p +\mu r}{\lambda q + \mu s} \leq \frac{r}{s} \\ \iff \quad &\frac{p}{q}-\frac{r}{s}\leq\frac{\lambda p +\mu r}{\lambda q + \mu s} -\frac{r}{s}\leq 0 \\ \iff \quad &\frac{ps-rq}{qs} \leq \frac{\lambda ps + \mu rs -\lambda rq -\mu rs}{\lambda qs +\mu s^2} \leq 0 \\ \iff \quad &\frac{-1}{qs} \leq \frac{-\lambda}{\lambda qs +\mu s^2} \leq 0 \\ \iff \quad &\frac{-1}{qs} \leq \frac{-1}{qs+\frac{\mu}{\lambda}s^2} \leq 0 \end{align} which is true, since all \(\lambda, \mu, q,s\) are positive. □

It what follows, it may be useful to note that the hypothesis in the previous Lemma could be written as:

\[ \det \begin{pmatrix} p & r \\ q & s \end{pmatrix}=-1 \]
Theorem 2.1 (Interval Characterization)
Let \(\frac{p}{q}<\frac{r}{s}\) be two rational numbers satisfying \(ps-rq=-1.\) Then a rational number \(\frac{a}{b}\) is an element of the interval \(\lbrack\frac{p}{q},\frac{r}{s}\rbrack\) if and only if \(a=\lambda p + \mu r \quad \text{ and } \quad b=\lambda q + \mu s \) for some non-negative integers \(\lambda\) and \(\mu.\)
Theorem 2.2 (Determinant Property)
Let \(\frac{p}{q}<\frac{r}{s}\) be any two consecutive fractions in \(\mathfrak{F}_n.\) Then \[\det \begin{pmatrix} p & r\\ q & s \end{pmatrix} =-1\]
Theorem 2.3 (Mediant Property)
The fractions that belong to \(\mathfrak{F}_n\) but not \(\mathfrak{F}_{n-1}\) are mediants of two consecutive elements of \(\mathfrak{F}_n.\)

2.2 Ford Circles

We take a slight detour from analysing the elements of the Farey Sequences in an arithmetic sense, and take a more geometrical approach, namely via Ford Circles. They are a different representation of the Farey Sequences and can give some insight to how well we can approximate arbitrary real numbers.

Definition 2.2 (Ford Circle)
For a rational number \(\frac{p}{q}\), we define the circle \(C(\frac{p}{q})\) in the complex plane having centre at \(\frac{p}{q}+\frac{1}{2q^2}i\) and radius \(\frac{1}{2q^2}.\) Such circles are called Ford Circles.

We see that the Ford Circles pack together peculiarly; one might make a few observations about them, and we provide proofs of those observations.

Theorem 2.4 (Ford Circle Non-intersection)
The intersection of the interiors of any two distinct Ford Circles is empty.
Proof:
It suffices to show that the distance between any two circles is greater than the sum of their radii. WLOG: we assume that \(\frac{p}{q}<\frac{r}{s}.\) We aim to show that \[ \sqrt{(\frac{p}{q}-\frac{r}{s})^2+(\frac{1}{2q^2}-\frac{1}{2s^2})^2} \geq \frac{1}{2q^2}+\frac{1}{2s^2} \] Which is equivalent to \begin{align} &(\frac{p}{q}-\frac{r}{s})^2+(\frac{1}{2q^2}-\frac{1}{2s^2})^2 \geq (\frac{1}{2q^2}+\frac{1}{2s^2})^2 \\ \iff &(\frac{p}{q}-\frac{r}{s})^2 \geq \frac{1}{s^2q^2} \\ \iff &(ps-rq)^2 \geq 1 \end{align} and this is true since all \(p,q,r,s\) are non-negative integers. □
Theorem 2.5 (Ford Circle Tangency)
Two Ford Circles, \(C(\frac{p}{q}) \text{ and } C(\frac{r}{s}),\) are tangent if and only if \(\frac{p}{q}\) and \(\frac{r}{s}\) are adjacent elements in \(\mathfrak{F}_n\) for some \(N.\)

In fact, we can use the Ford Circles to find excellent rational approximations to \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}.\) One way we can do this is by starting with the complex plane together with all the Ford Circles tangent to the real axis. Then we draw a ray perpendicular to the real axis starting from \(\alpha\) approaching to \(+\infty\). This ray will intersect an infinite sequence of shrinking Ford Circles. The points of tangency of those circles to the x-axis form a sequence of rational numbers approaching \(\alpha,\) all having relatively small height. The upper bound of the error will be \(\frac{1}{2q^2},\) the radius of the Ford Circles.
